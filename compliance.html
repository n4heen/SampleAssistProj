Compliance

Health Privacy Principles
HPP5 manages the storage of personal health information held by health services, it requires them to keep data for no longer than is necessary for the purpose of lawfully be used, information is disposed securely, and information must be protected by safeguards to ensure that information will not be used without authorisation, modified or disclosed.

Hard Copy Health Records
It is important to store health records in lockable storages or in secure access areas. Ensuring no health records are stored in public areas and not to be left unattended on benches or places accessible to unauthorised people.

Access at Patient Bedside
Health records at the patient's bedside are confidential and should not be accessed when a clinical staff is not present.

Disposal
According to the State Records Act any personal health information should be disposed of by shredding or pulping.Large amount of disposable information should be handled with specialised services and certificates of disposal obtained from the contractor should be provided.

Images and Photography

  • Privacy rules apply to personal health information where the identity of an individual is reasonably ascertained.
  • Images should be appropriate such as the level of resolution and quality must meet the clinical purpose.
  • If images are used for treatment or urgent care via a personal device, the image must be permanently deleted from the personal device afterwards.
  • If images or audio-visual images are reproduced into hard copies, secure storage, access to, use and disclosure of must be ensured.
  • Personal Smartphones used to capture images of patients for non-clinic purposes are not permitted.


Computer Systems and Applications
  • Electronic health records must only be accessed, viewed and used for only with intention related to their work.
  • USB, CD or laptops should be limited to employer owned media.
  • Password entry should be implemented into documents or systems

Disposal
  • Disposal of health records should be done to an unreadable and unreconstructed state.
  • Ensuring hard disk drives (HDD) from redundant PCs should be disposed of securely and safely by, or on behalf of the health service and a certificate of Destruction should be retained to confirm secure destruction.
Safeguards When delivering and Transmitting information
  • Ensuring the purpose of use or disclosure is authorised by HPP 10 (use) or HPP 11 (disclosure).
Use of Email
  • Emails are not to replace the recording of personal health information in electronic or paper health records and should be incorporated instead.
  • Emails they include personal health information should in could “Confidential” as the subject.
  • Making sure that the information stored is correct and is filed against the correct patient.
  • Ensuring personal health information is information that is necessary to communicate.
  • Double checking
  • Caution when using the “Reply All” function, making sure appropriate content is shared to the correct recipient.
  • Implementing password-protection or encryption

Europe GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

The Europe GDPR was designed to replace the previous 1995 data protection directive. GDPR is the data that allows an individual to be identified whether this personal data could be racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, genetic and biometric data, health information and data on an individual’s sex life or orientation. Hence this regulation is crucial in protecting personal data as the law covers individuals, organisations and companies that constitute personal data.

GDPR 7 Principles

  1. Lawfulness, Fairness and transparency
  2. Purpose limitation
  3. Data minimisation
  4. Accuracy
  5. Storage limitation
  6. Integrity and Confidentiality
  7. Accountability

How does this apply to Australian Organisations?

The European GDPR still applies to Australian Organisations when they have established a presence in the EU, such as launching a website or online presence, which means they need to comply with the regulations. Businesses should determine whether they need to comply with the GDPR and take necessary steps to ensure that personal data handling practices comply before any processes. From a healthcare perspective, compliance is essential if they handle the personal data of EU residents or offer services to them as they are protected under the GDPR law which also applies to monitoring their behaviour and data transfer internationally. Another reason this applies to organisations is that EU citizens may be in Australia for various reasons such as permanent visas, temporary visas or travel visas. Personal information for these individuals will be needed such as medical history for health organisations and this information may be found across different systems and databases stored in the EU.

Data Encryption Standards

AES256

Advanced Encryption Standards is a symmetric encryption algorithm best known for its high resistance to brute force attacks. This standard is used for secure data transmission and storage. AES 256 is widely used in healthcare as an encryption method for securing extensive databases that may contain patient files involving their medical histories and other personal information. This ensures that all sensitive data and information are kept confidential and prevents unauthorised access. This complies with the HIPAA standards in the lawful use and disclosure of protected health information to ensure sensitive patient information is protected and secured. AES uses symmetric key encryption meaning that it uses the same key for encrypting and decrypting data and offers 128, 192 or 256-bit key variants.


TLS 1.2/1.3 data transit

TLS (Transport Layer Security) 1.2 and 1.3 are cryptographic protocols designed to provide secure communication over a computer network. The main use for a TLS is to encrypt data in transit to ensure sensitive information is being transmitted securely across networks ensuring the data will be sent to the intended location without interception or tampering.

Organisations should be using TLS 1.3. This is because the vulnerable cipher suites found in 1.2 have been removed and a stronger cipher suite instead. Cipher Suites is a set of algorithms to help secure a connection.

Cipher Suites 3 different algorithms:

  • Key establishment for securely establishing a symmetric key between two devices
  • Bulk encryption for encrypting traffic
  • Message Authentication Code to ensure that the traffic is not modified in transit

Public Key Infrastructure

An encryption framework uses digital certificates to protect and authenticate communications between servers and users, these include securing websites, securing email messages and securing local networks. Digital certificates provide unique digital identities to users, applications and devices.

Generally, public key cryptography relies on 2 keys, one to encrypt and one to decrypt. The plaintext will be encrypted by the encryption key and encrypted into cipher text. This will be sent to the receiver, and they will need a decryption key to decode the textA certificate authority is a trusted third-party organisation that issues digital certificates to prove the corresponding certificate belongs to the certified public key. This process is important for organisations to establish trust in signatures, encryption and identities to secure websites, email signing, encryption and local networks.



FIPS 140-2/140-3 Compliance

FIPS, acronym for Federal Information Processing Standard, are guidelines released by NIST, outlining document processing, various encryption algorithms, and techniques for safeguarding sensitive information both physically and digitally. Verifying that cryptographic modules utilised to protect health data adhere to FIPS 140-2/140-3 standards ensures they meet strict global security criteria. It is crucial to ensure that patient information is protected from unauthorised access by encrypting data to the highest standards during both transit and at rest. Adhering to this requirement is crucial. Australian healthcare institutions demonstrate their commitment to safeguarding patient data and maintaining the confidentiality and integrity of sensitive health information through adherence to FIPS 140-2/140-3 regulations. Adherence to these standards guarantees that encryption methods meet strict security criteria, especially in safeguarding health data from unauthorised breaches and access.

Secure Cloud Storage

Secure cloud storage involves storing data on remote servers accessible online while ensuring protection against loss, theft, and unauthorised access. Common security measures in cloud storage include robust access controls, encryption, authentication processes, regular security assessments, and compliance with applicable laws and regulations. Health information stored in the cloud needs to be safeguarded from unauthorised access, loss, or exposure following Australian laws, such as the Australian Privacy Principles (APPs). When managing confidential medical information, cloud storage options must comply with FIPS 140-2/140-3 regulations to ensure encryption keys and cryptographic processes are secure and meet rigorous protection criteria.

Personal Data Protection Act (SINGAPORE)

What is classified as Personal Data?

Information that can be used to identify an individual whether it may be their gender, address, religion, beliefs or health records are classified as personal data.


What is PDPA?

PDPA or Personal Data Protection Act is a data protection standard for personal data in Singapore. It includes requirements for collection, use, disclosure and care of personal data in Singapore.


Objectives

The objective of the PDPA is to ensure organisations are collecting, using or disclosing personal data for legitimate and resale purposes. This ensures no data will be misused and can maintain an individual's trust in an organisation.

PDPA covers personal data stored electronically and non-electronic formats. However, it does not apply to:

  • Any individual acting on a personal or domestic basis.
  • Any individual acting in his/her capacity as an employee with an organisation.
  • Any public agency in relation to the collection, use or disclosure of personal data.
  • Business contact such as an individual’s name, position or title, business telephone number, business address, business email, business fax and similar information.

If an organisation wishes to use any personal data information such as to collect, use or disclose personal data they must comply with the data protection obligations.

Work Health and Safety (WHS) Act 2011

The purpose of the Work Health and Safety Act is to ensure that worker's health and safety in the workplace are assured, this could be achieved by implementing WHS practices and safety equipment. WHS provides long-term business benefits such as improved staff productivity, reduced injury, illness cost of injury and workers’ compensation.

  • Minimising risk by protecting workers and other people from harm
  • Fair and effective representation, consultation and cooperation
  • Encouraging unions and employers to promote WHS practices and improvements
  • Continuously using a framework for improvement and progressively achieving high work health and safety standards.
  • Maintaining and strengthening the national harmonisation of laws.

Different types of WHS names in Australia

  • NSW – Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (NSW)
  • VIC – Occupational Health and Safety Act 2004 (Vic)
  • QLD – Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld)
  • WA - Work Health and Safety Act 2020 (WA)
  • SA - Work Health and Safety Act 2012 (SA)
  • TAS - Work Health and Safety Act 2012 (Tas)
  • ACT - Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (ACT)
  • NT – Work Health and Safety Act (National Uniform Legislation) Act 2011 (NT)
  • Cwth – Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Cwth)

  4. Analysis (hazards vs. mitigation)
  5. Development of risk mitigation

  8. Monitoring
  9. Record keeping
  10. Reporting
